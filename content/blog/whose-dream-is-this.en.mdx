# Whose Dream Is This?

I grew up in an environment where nothing was in excess, but nothing essential was missing[cite: 1]. My parents did a great job providing a solid foundation[cite: 2]. However, outside—and sometimes inside—the house, the world took it upon itself to install a piece of software in me: the software of constant comparison[cite: 2].

The voice that breathed life into my insecurity was the closest one: *“Why don’t you get the grades they do?”, “Why are they getting awards and you aren’t?”, “Why can’t you be like them?”*[cite: 3, 4]. These questions, though likely born from a desire to see me succeed, planted a dangerous idea: that my value was conditional[cite: 4]. I learned early on that being good wasn’t enough; I had to be better than the person next to me to earn the pride of those I loved most[cite: 4].

Later, the education system formalized that training as a race with a leaderboard[cite: 4]. The irony is that, for a long time, I wasn't even good at winning those trophies I didn't want[cite: 5]. I graduated with a university degree, but the feeling of inadequacy remained intact[cite: 5]. I had simply checked a box on a wishlist that wasn't mine[cite: 5].

## The Bank Account Scoreboard

It wasn't until I entered the professional world that I finally felt like the pieces fit[cite: 6]. When I started earning more than many of those who were once "the best," I felt like I had finally surpassed them[cite: 7]. My salary became my first real trophy[cite: 8].

However, over time I realized that money became my **refuge against uncertainty**. In a world that always made me feel insufficient, accumulating became my way of building armor. But I fell into a circular trap: when I don't have enough, I am consumed by anxiety; but when I do have it, I feel just as bad because the goalpost always moves and it never seems to be enough. I have lived under the dictatorship of a number, believing that peace of mind could be bought, when in reality I was only building a glass fortress that is never quite high enough[cite: 10].

This blind search for security has collateral victims. Amidst this noise, I want to ask my partner for forgiveness. I know that my indecision, my shadows, and the torment of not knowing which dreams to follow have been a heavy burden for her. It is not easy to walk alongside someone who is still trying to put out internal fires, and I deeply regret that my lack of clarity sometimes clouds our present. Thank you for your patience while I try to separate who I am from who I was told I should be.

## The Algorithm and the Silence

Today, comparison doesn't come from a familiar voice, but from an algorithm designed to exploit our need to stand out[cite: 12, 27]. It is a losing battle because I am comparing my "behind the scenes" with someone else's gala premiere[cite: 15]. That is why I created this space: to expose the reality behind what we filter[cite: 16].

At "22 dreams" old, I took my first flight and my world began to expand[cite: 17, 18]. During my travels, I experienced something strange: I felt enough[cite: 19]. Being in a different culture gave me a satisfaction that didn't need validation[cite: 20]. But when I return to the screen, that sufficiency vanishes, and the nostalgia of ignorance hits me[cite: 21].

I constantly ask myself how to break these chains. I want to know how to stop letting money dictate my world. I want it to be just a tool and not the architect of my identity. I want to stop allowing my value to rise or fall based on a bank balance.

Today I stop and ask myself: **Whose dream is this?**[cite: 28]. Is it mine, my parents', the system's, or the algorithm's?[cite: 29]. I admit I don't have the answer[cite: 29]. I don't yet know what my real dreams are, but I am starting by identifying which ones definitely are not, so I can finally let them go[cite: 30]. Perhaps the way back begins by silencing all those voices to discover what is left of me when no one is comparing me to anyone else[cite: 31, 32].
